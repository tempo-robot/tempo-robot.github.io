Closing the Representational Gap for VLAs in Dynamic Settings

Anonymous Authors

Abstract

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models are conditioned on a single observation at inference time, a design that suffices for quasi-static manipulation but creates two representational failures in dynamic settings. The first is motion ambiguity: a snapshot encodes configuration, not how the scene is evolving, so the policy cannot anticipate where a moving target will be. The second is state aliasing: perceptually similar observations at different task phases can demand opposite actions, producing jitter and mistimed commitments even on tasks the policy nominally understands.

We show that these two failures are separable and that each has a natural fix. A motion summary from a frozen video foundation model separates scene dynamics from appearance without task-specific supervision, while a compact proprioceptive history disambiguates sequential task phases without per-task engineering. TEMPO adds these as minimal inputs to a pretrained VLA without architectural modification, together adding only 2M parameters (0.08% overhead) and leaving the backbone entirely intact.

Method

TEMPO adds two compact temporal signals to a frozen VLA, each fixing one failure mode with near-zero overhead and no architectural change. TEMPOMOT is an object-agnostic motion token from a frozen video foundation model that summarizes how the scene is moving, fixing motion ambiguity. TEMPOACT is a compact summary of the robot's recent joint history that tells the policy which task phase it is in, fixing state aliasing. Motion encoding runs in a background thread, so it adds essentially no inference-time cost.

Paper: Sec. 3 (TEMPOMOT Sec. 3.1, TEMPOACT Sec. 3.2), Fig. 2; latency Tab. 1, Sec. 3.4.

Analysis

The Policy Attends to Each Signal When It Matters

We visualize the action expert's attention mass over a representative Flick Catch episode. Attention on TEMPOMOT rises as the human hand starts moving, corresponding to major movement in the observation, while TEMPOACT steadily gains attention during the hand- following phase and drops back down after the ball is caught. The two signals are consulted at distinct moments: motion during tracking, proprioceptive history at commitment.

Paper: Fig. 6 (attention), Sec. 5.

State Aliasing in Decision Making

Why does a single-frame policy stall before it even starts? Because the same image recurs at opposite moments of the task. In the real episodes below, the arm passes through the same configuration once on the way in and once on the way out: the two highlighted frames are near-identical (their visual similarity, measured by a frozen vision encoder, is shown in the middle) yet demand opposite actions. State aliasing in action. The approach and retract phases of the gripper produce nearly identical observations, so VLASH, conditioned on a single frame, cannot tell them apart and oscillates between reaching and withdrawing, failing to even start the task. This is the artifact that drives both RTC and VLASH to 0% on the state-aliased Wine Pour and Flick Catch tasks. TEMPO completely resolves it: with a compact proprioceptive history, the policy knows which phase it is in and the gripper approaches and retracts normally, as shown in the state-aliasing success rollout above.
