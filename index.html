Abstract

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models are conditioned on a single observation at inference time, a design that suffices for quasi-static manipulation but creates two representational failures in dynamic settings. The first is motion ambiguity: a snapshot encodes configuration, not how the scene is evolving, so the policy cannot anticipate where a moving target will be. The second is state aliasing: perceptually similar observations at different task phases can demand opposite actions, producing jitter and mistimed commitments even on tasks the policy nominally understands.

We show that these two failures are separable and that each has a natural fix. A motion summary from a frozen video foundation model separates scene dynamics from appearance without task-specific supervision, while a compact proprioceptive history disambiguates sequential task phases without per-task engineering. TEMPO adds these as minimal inputs to a pretrained VLA without architectural modification, together adding only 2M parameters (0.08% overhead) and leaving the backbone entirely intact.

On four dynamic bimanual tasks, TEMPO improves Bottle Handover success from 44% to 74% and is the only system to complete the full versions of Wine Pour and Flick Catch, where strong asynchronous baselines score 0% due to unresolved state aliasing. Probing and ablation analyses confirm that each signal is individually necessary, suggesting that what limits VLAs in dynamic settings is not model capacity but the right temporal context.

Dynamic Tasks

We evaluate on a bimanual platform across four dynamic tasks designed to expose two ambiguities in isolation and combination. Each task is dynamic and shares four properties that a current-frame observation alone cannot resolve: (P1) the target object is moving; (P2) the relevant target shifts during the episode; (P3) the task decomposes into a sequence of subtasks; and (P4) the policy faces state ambiguity, where a perceptually identical state may demand opposite actions depending on what came before.

Method

TEMPO architecture. TEMPO augments a VLM-based policy with two inputs derived from recent history. A general motion encoder compresses a window of past observations into a compact object-motion token that encodes global scene flow, while the robot's recent proprioceptive history is mean-pooled into a compact summary of past joint states. The action expert cross-attends each hidden layer of the VLM representation; the proprioceptive summary conditions the denoising computation through adaptive RMS normalization, gating each layer's attention and MLP norms with learned scale factors.

TEMPO targets the two representational failure modes with two compact signals, each closing a distinct gap and attaching to a pretrained VLA as a plug-and-play addition with near-zero overhead.

TEMPOMOT: Scene Motion Encoding

TEMPOMOT summarizes how the scene has been moving, resolving motion ambiguity for fast, shifting targets. A motion-focused encoding does not emerge from stacking frames alone: a model compressing a near-static window still spends most of its capacity on appearance. We instead source the motion code from encoders purpose-built to isolate motion, either by decoupling it from scene structure or by training on dense pixel correspondence and optical flow. The encoder is kept object-agnostic: it operates on the full frame with no detector, tracker, or per-frame prompt, so a single fixed encoder transfers across tasks and to unseen objects. We instantiate it with a frozen pretrained video foundation model in streaming mode, applied off the shelf with no motion-specific fine-tuning; the backbone is a swappable component.

TEMPOACT: Proprioceptive History Encoding

Where TEMPOMOT resolves what is moving, TEMPOACT resolves what the robot has been doing. A compact summary of recent joint history gives the policy the temporal context it needs to commit to the correct task phase rather than hedging at each ambiguous frame. Raw joint history is long, high-frequency, and largely redundant, so we partition the most recent joint positions into contiguous time buckets and summarize each bucket by its mean. The resulting summary is fixed-size regardless of how far back it reaches: extending the horizon changes the bucket length, not the token count. The aggregation is parameter-free and keeps the low-frequency trend that encodes the end-effector's recent motion, while adding negligible context and compute.

Integration and Real-Time Deployment

The motion code is projected to a single token placed in the VLM input alongside the current observation and the language instruction, where the action expert reads it by cross-attention. The proprioceptive bucket tokens are mean-pooled into a conditioning vector that gates each action-expert layer's normalization, reaching the action expert directly without passing through the VLM token stream. The added projections are zero-initialized, so fine-tuning starts from the pretrained policy and reuses its original flow-matching objective unchanged, with no auxiliary loss and no new prediction head.

To keep motion encoding off the critical path, we run it in a dedicated background thread, fully decoupled from the control loop. The encoder consumes camera frames at sensor rate and continuously refreshes a shared snapshot of the latest motion tokens; each control tick the policy reads only this snapshot, so the encoder's cost is hidden behind policy inference rather than added to it. The only latency the policy actually pays is the cost of attending over a few extra prefix tokens.

Analysis

The Policy Attends to Each Signal When It Matters

We visualize the action expert's attention mass over a representative Flick Catch episode. Attention on TEMPOMOT rises as the human hand starts moving, corresponding to major movement in the observation, while TEMPOACT steadily gains attention during the hand- following phase and drops back down after the ball is caught. The two signals are consulted at distinct moments: motion during tracking, proprioceptive history at commitment. Probing the policy's hidden state further confirms it encodes object velocity far better than the baselines on the tasks that most demand it (notably Flick Catch and Wine Pour), evidence that TEMPO supplies a motion signal the policy actually uses.


When a Single Frame Is Not Enough

State aliasing in action. The task is to grip the velcro plate and follow the human hand. A single-frame baseline cannot differentiate reaching out for the plate from releasing it, because the two phases produce nearly identical observations, so it fails to even start the task. This is the artifact that drives both asynchronous baselines to 0% on the full Wine Pour and Flick Catch tasks.
